On a certain day in a certain summer, I had just graduated from school. I went back to my village to greet my parents. My time in the city was finally over, and I could finally relax for a few moments.

I came to my room, tidied by my mother. Everything was in place — my desk was clean, my books were organized, and my old toys were stuffed in the closet.

As the nostalgia crept into my mind, I had an idea: to go through the toys and memories I had left behind. I took them out of the closet, the fond memories of when I was a child. I laughed, bitter and sweet all at once, at my childhood days. It was a happy moment.

In the back of all the things I used to cherish, a small, old box was there, as if greeting me. I took it out, only to see a childish handwriting scrawled above the lid. *Do not open!*

What was it again? I thought. It must have been very long ago. I didn't think much of it, and opened the lid. Inside was a sunflower that had already withered. Looking at it, I remembered a small, tiny time, even before these priceless toys of the past — a memory of me and an old friend of mine.

When I was a little kid back in my rural hometown, there were sunflowers inside the forest where we used to play. I was accompanied by a girl I'd met there. I took no interest in playing outside until she suddenly showed up one summer holiday.

"Hey you! Let's play outside!"

She was always the one to approach me. Come on, don't be so gloomy. Life is no fun if you don't enjoy it. Boo~. She'd say that to me almost every time we played.

She was a kind girl. She would play with the gloomy me. She would often share her food whenever she was in a good mood.

Yet she was rash — she would punch me for little reasons whenever she was in a bad mood. She'd pull me outside from my humble home. Come on! Come outside. She'd lightly punch me and drag me by the feet sometimes, which somehow reminded me of how I used to be scared of her back then.

I followed her wherever she went. I learned how to be happy, how to enjoy life to the fullest, how to go on an adventure, and how to enjoy seeing the beautiful sunflowers.

But then, that girl left.

"I am moving."

She visited me once more before she went away with her family. She gave me something — a box. A closed box, only for me.

"I'll give you this box as a farewell gift. Open it when you really miss me!"

"Sure…"

She climbed into the car, waving her hand out the window.

"Bye-bye!"

Come to think of it, what expression did she make that time?

After that, I tried not to remember her farewell gift, and hid it in my closet, so that I wouldn't look back, and could become a stronger person one day.

Inside the box, it felt like something more was stuffed within. I scratched at its surface and found that its floor was actually another lid, hiding a deeper part. So I opened it.

Oof — there we go. "Long time no see."

I jolted back. Gahh?! She came right out of the box.

Looking exactly the same as when she was a child, she was angry.

"You completely forgot about the box I gave you, didn't you?!"

She looked at me with a deadly stare.

"Geez, it's only your body that's gotten bigger!"

Startled, all I could do was mutter, "...Sorry."

She stood there looking at me as I stared back. She looked so small now, compared to how big I'd gotten.

She nodded, smirking. "Alright."

"I'll grant you three wishes!"

"Come again…?"

"Isn't that what usually happens in this kind of situation." Hehehe.

She acted like a genie, waving her hand as if conjuring something supernatural. In my mind, I thought, she hadn't changed one bit.

"Go ahead, say anything you want."

Anything I want? Well, the first thing that came to mind was,

"Give me lots of money."

She took something out of her pocket.

"Okay. Here's two 500 yen coins."

"What? That's only 1,000 yen."

A thousand yen was basically nothing, let alone enough to be rich. Uhh…

"So what?! You can still splash out at Granny's Candy store with two 500 yen coins!"

She said, her face reddening. Also, Granny's candy store had closed down years ago… As I thought, some things about her would never change.

"Okay, and your second wish is?"

Well, the next thing that came to mind after money was pretty obvious. I am a man, after all.

"Um… then I want a pretty girl."

It didn't take her long to fluster and kick me down. She locked my arm and pressed her legs against my back. Even though she was still a child, it still hurt. I knew this would happen.

"You have me, don't you?!"

"Aww! Gahhh!"

I lay there, catching my breath. Huff…huff…

"Um, so, for your last wish…"

"Yeah…?"

"I want you to grant my wish."

As usual, she was being rash and unreasonable. What's that about? I thought. Isn't this wish supposed to be mine?

"My wish is an easy one. I want you to dig a hole."

I stared at her, confused for a moment, then let out a breath. Haa…

"…Sure, I'll do it."

"All right! Go! Go! Let's go!"

She threw her hand up to the sky and hurriedly dragged me outside.

We went to the forest where we used to play as kids. I couldn't see any sunflowers at the time — maybe because they hadn't bloomed yet.

I quickly put my shovel to work and dug a hole just as she wished — a fairly big one.

"Is this good?"

"Nope, it has to be wider."

Just how big did she want the hole to be, anyway?

"Put your heart into it and dig."

"Sure…got it."

She crossed her arms, as if to show who was boss.

After a while,

"Okay, this looks good!"

"Mmm, okay…"

She climbed into the hole and lay down, arms crossed over her belly.

"Yeah, this fits perfectly! You did a great job."

"…Thanks."

Then came a moment of silence, just me staring at her, and her staring back.

"What are you doing?"

Her words broke me out of my empty state of mind.

"Cover me with dirt."

"Oh… sure."

As I covered her small legs and body, she looked at me intently — emotionless, but somehow caring.

"Hey you. Listen up."

I didn't stop, slowly burying her body as I listened.

"I want you to think of this as my final request, rather than a wish."

She paused.

"No matter what happens, don't cry. Live."

After hearing that, I stopped. All that was left was her head, her eyes looking up at me. I returned her stare, as if giving my resolve.

"Now, hurry up and cover me."

"Okay…"

She closed her eyes, waiting to be covered. I started moving my shovel again, slowly covering her face until she was finally gone.

In that moment, I felt rain in my eyes as tears slowly fell down my face. The pale sky suddenly turned red, the sun bidding farewell, as if ending the day.

The next day, I decided to visit the forest once more. I bought flowers, and brought the two 500 yen coins she had given me.

The view was different. Yesterday there had been no flowers at all. Today, a rainbow of colors spread across the field inside the forest — a beautiful sight.

Before I met her, I cried a lot, failed at everything, and convinced myself that I was a failure. But her words, her voice, stopped me from thinking that way. I realized how tiny my worries really were.

The day she moved away was the day she died in a car accident.

I could still remember her smile before she left. It wasn't the cold stare or the angry ones she usually greeted me with. It was a sincere, beautiful smile, her eyes crinkled — the last smile she ever gave me.

I have to keep her last words. ...I loved you! Even now, you will still be here in my heart.

I will stay strong… And live.

The flowers I had bought, I spread across that same field. In the middle of it, beside the hole I once dug, sunflowers greeted me.

Soon, it will bloom too — the *Sunflower* I planted…
